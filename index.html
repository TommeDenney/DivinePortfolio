About

Rooted in field, community,
& science

Divine is a graduate student in the MS Climate program at Columbia University's Climate School, where she is also a Student Council Representative for the Columbia Climate Graduate Council. She is currently a Research Assistant at Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory, contributing to MacArthur Fellow Dr. Kristina Douglass's Olo Be Taloha Lab.

Her work spans coastal ecology, transdisciplinary research methods, and community-based approaches to climate adaptation. She integrates Local, Indigenous, and Descendant (LID) knowledge systems with geospatial, archaeological, and oral history data to understand how communities adapt to environmental change — particularly in coastal Southwest Madagascar.

A CERF Rising TIDES Scholar and Columbia Merit Scholar, Divine brings fieldwork experience across Massachusetts, Cambodia, Florida, and Madagascar, and a deep commitment to ethical data stewardship and knowledge sovereignty.

2022Research Paper

Trophic Cascades & Imbalance in Coastal Ecosystems

The Combined Impacts of Climate Change, Overfishing, and Predation Loss on Sesarma Reticulatum and Salt Marsh Stability

This project examines how climate change, overfishing, and predator loss interact to destabilize coastal salt marsh ecosystems through trophic cascades involving the herbivorous crab Sesarma reticulatum. Focused on Waquoit Bay NERR, the research traces how Sesarma's burrowing and grazing behaviors weaken the root systems of Spartina alterniflora, leading to erosion, plant die-off, and increased vulnerability to sea-level rise.
